    <title>News story: CDE Innovation Network event: 1 October 2015, London</title>
    <category label="News story" term="News story"/>
    <summary>The Centre for Defence Enterprise (CDE) held a networking event for science and technology providers in London on 1 October 2015.</summary>
    <content type="html">&lt;p&gt;&lt;em&gt;Updated:&lt;/em&gt; Presentation slides now added. &lt;/p&gt;&lt;div class="govspeak"&gt;&lt;p&gt;The event took place in London at the &lt;a rel="external" href="http://www.grandconnaughtrooms.com"&gt;Grand Connaught Rooms&lt;/a&gt;, 9:30am to 4pm.&lt;/p&gt;

&lt;p&gt;&lt;a href="https://www.gov.uk/government/people/jim-pennycook"&gt;Jim Pennycook&lt;/a&gt;, Head of Operations at &lt;abbr title="Centre for Defence Enterprise"&gt;CDE&lt;/abbr&gt;,  introduced &lt;abbr title="Centre for Defence Enterprise"&gt;CDE&lt;/abbr&gt; and gave an overview of proof-of-concept research funding opportunities for innovative science and technology providers. Technical experts provided specific information about the &lt;a href="https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/cde-themed-competition-security-for-the-internet-of-things"&gt;themed competition: security for the internet of things&lt;/a&gt;. This competition has up to £1 million available for fully funded phase-1 projects. &lt;/p&gt;

&lt;p&gt;&lt;a rel="external" href="http://www.slideshare.net/MOD_CDE/am-briefing-security-for-the-internet-of-things"&gt;View the presentation slides from the morning session&lt;/a&gt;.&lt;/p&gt;

&lt;p&gt;In addition, Bruce Hardie, &lt;abbr title="Centre for Defence Enterprise"&gt;CDE&lt;/abbr&gt;’s Technology Manager, will talk about how to create an effective proposal for &lt;abbr title="Centre for Defence Enterprise"&gt;CDE&lt;/abbr&gt; funding.&lt;/p&gt;

&lt;p&gt;&lt;a rel="external" href="http://www.slideshare.net/MOD_CDE/pm-briefing-security-for-the-internet-of-things"&gt;View the presentation slides from the afternoon session&lt;/a&gt;.&lt;/p&gt;

&lt;p&gt;Attendees were able to pre-book a face-to-face meeting with &lt;abbr title="Centre for Defence Enterprise"&gt;CDE&lt;/abbr&gt; and subject matter experts to discuss potential proposals for the ‘security for the internet of things’ themed competition.&lt;/p&gt;

&lt;p&gt;There were networking opportunities with other potential bidders, current &lt;abbr title="Defence Science and Technology Laboratory"&gt;Dstl&lt;/abbr&gt; suppliers, &lt;abbr title="Centre for Defence Enterprise"&gt;CDE&lt;/abbr&gt;, &lt;abbr title="Defence Science and Technology Laboratory"&gt;Dstl&lt;/abbr&gt; and military experts.&lt;/p&gt;

&lt;h2 id="about-cde"&gt;About &lt;abbr title="Centre for Defence Enterprise"&gt;CDE&lt;/abbr&gt;
&lt;/h2&gt;

&lt;p&gt;&lt;abbr title="Centre for Defence Enterprise"&gt;CDE&lt;/abbr&gt; funds novel, high-risk, high-potential-benefit research. We work with the broadest possible range of science and technology providers, including academia and small companies, to develop cost-effective capabilities for UK armed forces and national security.&lt;/p&gt;

&lt;p&gt;&lt;abbr title="Centre for Defence Enterprise"&gt;CDE&lt;/abbr&gt; is part of &lt;a href="https://www.gov.uk/government/organisations/defence-science-and-technology-laboratory"&gt;&lt;abbr title="Defence Science and Technology Laboratory"&gt;Dstl&lt;/abbr&gt;&lt;/a&gt;.&lt;/p&gt;

    <title>Press release: Dstl adds to open source software</title>
    <category label="Press release" term="Press release"/>
    <summary>The Defence Science and Technology Laboratory (Dstl) looks for collaborators for its latest software released into the open source domain.</summary>
    <content type="html">&lt;div class="govspeak"&gt;&lt;p&gt;The entity extraction framework – known as Baleen – can automatically extract information from unstructured and semi-structured text. It tries to identify and extract entities from the text, such as people, locations, organisations and dates.&lt;/p&gt;

&lt;p&gt;Baleen has been under development for a number of years; &lt;abbr title="Defence Science and Technology Laboratory"&gt;Dstl&lt;/abbr&gt; is now seeking community contributions that can feed back into the software, improving the quality of the code and extending its capability.&lt;/p&gt;

&lt;p&gt;There are similar projects already in the public domain, but this provides an end-to-end entity extraction capability based on Apache &lt;abbr title="Unstructured Information Management Architecture"&gt;UIMA&lt;/abbr&gt; (Unstructured Information Management Architecture) which is becoming a widely used and accepted framework. &lt;/p&gt;

&lt;p&gt;&lt;abbr title="Defence Science and Technology Laboratory"&gt;Dstl&lt;/abbr&gt;’s James Baker says he hopes the text analytics developer community will help develop the software further:&lt;/p&gt;

&lt;blockquote&gt;
  &lt;p class="last-child"&gt;We are releasing the core framework and a number of components of Baleen onto Github.com for the community to use, adapt and improve. We hope suppliers, members of academia and individuals will help take this further and develop capabilities which we have not yet uncovered, as well as find a use for it in their own work.&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;/blockquote&gt;

&lt;p&gt;&lt;abbr title="Defence Science and Technology Laboratory"&gt;Dstl&lt;/abbr&gt;’s code can be found on the &lt;a rel="external" href="http://github.com/dstl/baleen"&gt;Github site&lt;/a&gt;. For further information on the technical side, email: &lt;a href="mailto:oss@dstl.gov.uk?Subject=Baleen%20Enquiry%20via%20GOV.UK"&gt;oss@dstl.gov.uk&lt;/a&gt;.&lt;/p&gt;

    <title>Press release: New research shows need for emergency responders to carry blood</title>
    <category label="Press release" term="Press release"/>
    <summary>Dstl research shows that using blood products to treat severely injured patients before they reach hospital improves chances of survival. </summary>
    <content type="html">&lt;div class="govspeak"&gt;&lt;p&gt;New research carried out by Ministry of Defence (&lt;abbr title="Ministry of Defence"&gt;MOD&lt;/abbr&gt;) scientists at the Defence Science and Technology Laboratory (&lt;abbr title="Defence Science and Technology Laboratory"&gt;Dstl&lt;/abbr&gt;), with the Royal Centre for Defence Medicine (&lt;abbr title="Royal Centre for Defence Medicine"&gt;RCDM&lt;/abbr&gt;), has demonstrated the need for emergency first responders to carry blood products. The research has provided the first evidence that the use of blood products (red blood cells and plasma) to treat severely injured patients before they reach hospital significantly improves their chances of survival. &lt;/p&gt;

&lt;p&gt;In 2008 the medical evacuation response team (&lt;abbr title="medical evacuation response team"&gt;MERT&lt;/abbr&gt;) in Afghanistan started carrying blood products to injured personnel on the frontline thanks to the development of special refrigeration units on the Chinook helicopters. This emergency care procedure, coupled with other measures, has been credited with saving a number of lives in Afghanistan. &lt;/p&gt;

&lt;p&gt;Dr Malcolm Russell MBE is the Medical Director of the Kent, Surrey &amp;amp; Sussex Air Ambulance Trust; he is also an ex-military doctor, and former Senior Lecturer in Military Pre-hospital Emergency Medicine. Dr Russell says: &lt;/p&gt;

&lt;blockquote&gt;
  &lt;p class="last-child"&gt;Clinical experience from military operations over the last 10-15 years has suggested that administering blood products to patients who have suffered substantial blood loss due to major trauma ought to be helpful in reducing shock and improving survival. Two years ago, Kent, Surrey &amp;amp; Sussex Air Ambulance took the decision to carry packed red blood cells on our helicopters in our quest to improve the outcome for our civilian major trauma patients. Earlier this year, we were the first UK air ambulance to carry freeze-dried plasma as well as packed red blood cells and we carefully balance the use of these products as we resuscitate our major trauma patients.&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;/blockquote&gt;

&lt;p&gt;The latest &lt;abbr title="Defence Science and Technology Laboratory"&gt;Dstl&lt;/abbr&gt; and &lt;abbr title="Royal Centre for Defence Medicine"&gt;RCDM&lt;/abbr&gt; research proves the benefits of administering blood products to severely injured patients before they reach hospital and provides much-needed evidence for the continued use of this procedure. It could also have an impact for civilian first responders such as ambulance and air ambulance crew. Currently only a few air ambulance services in the UK have the mandate, staff and systems required to carry blood products. &lt;/p&gt;

&lt;p&gt;Dr Russell continues: &lt;/p&gt;

&lt;blockquote&gt;
  &lt;p class="last-child"&gt;We feel that the administration of blood products has been really helpful for major trauma patients, though proving that is very difficult in practice. Consequently, we were lacking the objective evidence that is essential to consolidate the clinical practice and bring it into wider use. We therefore welcome this excellent piece of research which now provides exactly that evidence we’ve been looking for and which supports our clinical intuition, judgement and subjective evaluation of clinical evidence. This combined evidence now makes the case that giving blood products to this patient group gives real benefit to them and is therefore a practice that should be continued and developed further.&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;/blockquote&gt;

&lt;p&gt;Dr Emrys Kirkman, Principal Scientist at &lt;abbr title="Defence Science and Technology Laboratory"&gt;Dstl&lt;/abbr&gt; says: &lt;/p&gt;

&lt;blockquote&gt;
  &lt;p class="last-child"&gt;Badly injured people often lose the ability to form a blood clot properly, just when they need it most. Our research provides the scientific foundation for the premise that giving blood products before seriously injured patients reach hospital could help save lives as it improves the ability to form blood clots. While this represents emerging practice based on clinical common sense nobody knew whether it actually helped, this is the first study to prove the benefits. We hope that this research will encourage decision makers to think again about the possibilities for both military and civilian first responders to carry blood products.&lt;/p&gt;
&lt;/blockquote&gt;

&lt;blockquote&gt;

&lt;/blockquote&gt;

&lt;p&gt;Founded in 2009 and based in Malvern, Worcestershire, IQHQ develops state-of-the-art military-grade radio technologies. It has a range of patents and inventions in key technologies such as very high-resilience receiver design, spectrum management and through-wall penetration.&lt;/p&gt;
